About


Hello!


I'm Isabelle Langrock, a PhD candidate at the Annenberg School for Communication at the University of Pennsylvania. I research the ways that politics, identity, and technology are entwined in digital information environments. I'm particularly interested in how inequalities are recognized and sometimes reconciled within peer production projects -- from Wikipedia to open source software to academic citations. While still in its early stages, my dissertation project will examine the "gender gaps" in open knowledge projects and assess activist groups and social movements attempts to close them. I take a multi-method approach to my work, including thematic analysis, network analysis, and nlp. In general, I love learning and talking about new methods for research (especially computational ones!).


At Annenberg, I am a member of the Digital Media, Networks, and Political Communication (DiMeNET) Research Group, run by advisor Sandra González-Bailón. I'm also a member of the Democracy and Information Group (DIG). I've held positions on the Annenberg grad student council and on the planning committee for our annual grad symposium. In researching collaboration, I often seek out ways to build more into my academic life; I've coordinated summer reading groups and, most recently, a weekly accountability check-in/writing group for my cohort.


Originally from a tiny town in Vermont, I recieved a MA in Communication from the Annenberg School for Communication, the University of Pennsylvania and a BA in the History, Philosophy, and Social Studies of Science and Medicine (HiPSS) from the University of Chicago. Prior to grad school, I spent several years working for Girls Who Code, a not-for-profit dedicated to teaching girls and young women skills to succeed in the technology sector.
